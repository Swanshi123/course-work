Simple linear regression

  • A simple linear regression model defines the relationship between a dependent variable and a single independent predictor variable

\[ y=\alpha + \beta x +\varepsilon \]

  • The key task: finding out parameter estimates for \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\)

Ordinary least squares (OLS)

  • An estimation

  • In OLS regression, the slope and intercept are chosen such that they minimize the sum of the squared errors (SSE)

  • The errors (\(\hat{\varepsilon}\) known as residuals) are the vertical distance between the predicted y value (\(\hat{y}\)) and the actual y value \(y\)

    • \(\sum(y_i -\hat{y}_i)^2=\sum \hat{e}_i^2\)
    • it is also called resudual sum of squares (RSS)

  • The OLS method minimizes the sum of the squared errors

    • the minimizing values of \(\alpha\) and \(\beta\) are the OLS estimates

\[ \begin{array}{rl} \hat{\beta} &=& \frac{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})(y_i-\bar{y})}{\sum(x_i-\bar{x})^2} \\ \hat{\alpha} &=& \bar{y}-\hat{\beta}\bar{x} \end{array} \]

Because the errors can be over-estimates or under-estimates, they can be positive or negative values; squaring them makes the errors positive regardless of direction

Correlations

  • we can make a contrast of correlation in the context of regression

  • correlation typically refers to the Pearson correlation coefficient

  • The correlation between two variables is a number that indicates how closely their relationship follows a straight line

  • \(\rho_{x,y}=Corr(x,y)= \frac{Cov(x,y)}{\sigma_x \sigma_y}\)

  • âcorrelation does not imply causationâ

Assess the accuracy of the coefficient estimates

  • The standard error of the coefficient estimates is a measure of how much the coefficient estimates are likely to vary from the true population values

\[ SE(\hat{\beta}_1) =\sqrt{\frac{\sigma^2}{\sum^n_{i=1}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}}, \quad SE(\hat{\beta}_0)=\sqrt{\sigma^2 \left [ \frac{1}{n} +\frac{\bar{x}^2}{\sum^n_{i=1}(x_i-\bar{x})^2}\right ]} \]

\(\textrm{where} \; \sigma^2=Var(\varepsilon)\)

  • The standard error of an estimator reflects how it varies under repeated sampling
  • standard errors can be used to compute confidence intervals

\[ [\hat{\beta}_1-2 \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_1), \hat{\beta}_1+2 \cdot SE(\hat{\beta}_1)] \]

  • Also, \(R^2\)

\[ R^2 =1- \frac{RSS}{TSS}, \quad \textrm{where} \quad TSS=\sum^n_{i=1}(y_i-\bar{y})^2 \]

Multiple linear regression

  • A multiple regression model defines the relationship between a dependent variable and two or more independent predictor variables

\[ Y_i=\alpha + \beta_1 X_{2i} + \cdots + \beta_k X_{pi} +\varepsilon_i \]

  • how to interpret the coefficients, \(\beta_j\)

  • The formula could be shown by using matrix notation

\[\mathbf{Y = X\beta + \varepsilon} \]

  • the best estimate of the vector \(\mathbf{\beta}\)

\[\hat{\mathbf{\beta}}=(X^TX)^{-1}X^TY\]

An Example - launch

  • On January 28th, 1986, seven crew members of the United States space shuttle Challenger were killed when a rocket booster failed, causing a catastrophic disintegration

  • In the aftermath, experts quickly focused on the launch temperature as a potential culprit

  • The rubber O-rings responsible for sealing the rocket joints had never been tested below \(4C^o\), and the weather on launch day was unusually cold and below freezing

  • The rocket engineers almost certainly knew that cold temperatures could make the components more brittle and less able to seal properly, which would result in a higher chance of a dangerous fuel leak

  • A regression model that demonstrated a link between temperature and O-ring failures, and could forecast the chance of failure given the expected temperature at launch, might have been very helpful

More details on multiple regression

interactions and nonlinearity

  • interactions

    • the effect of one predictor variable on the dependent variable depends on the value of another predictor variable

  • nonlinearity

    • the relationship between the predictors and the dependent variable is not linear

  • in our previous advertising example, we assumed that the effect on sales of increasing one advertising medium is independent of the amount spent on the other media

    • \(\widehat{sales}=\hat{\alpha} + \hat{\beta}_1 \times TV + \hat{\beta}_2 \times Radio + \hat{\beta}_3 \times Newspaper\)

  • it could happen that given a fixed budget, instead of allocating the entire amount to either TV or to radio spending half on radio and half on TV may increase sales more

  • in marketing research, this is known as a synergy effect

    • we call interaction effect in statistics analysis

Generalized linear models and logistic regression

  • not every problem is well suited to being modeled by linear regression

  • the statistical assumptions made by regression models are violated in many real-world tasks

  • For modeling counting values, for categorical or binary outcomes, as well as other cases where the target is not a normally distributed continuous variable

    • standard linear regression is not the best tool for the job

  • Even the Challenger data is less than ideal for linear regression as it violates the regression assumption that the target variable is measured on a continuous scale

  • As the number of O-ring failures can only take countable values, it makes no sense that the model might predict exactly 2.21 distress events rather than either two or three

  • The GLM loosens two assumptions of traditional regression modeling:

    • it allows the target to be a non-continuous variable
    • it allows the variance of the target variable to be related to its mean (e.g.Â Poisson distribution)

GLM

Two key components of any GLM:

  1. the distribution of the target feature must be chosen from members of the exponential family of distributions
    • includes the normal distribution, and Poisson, Binomial, and Gamma
  2. The link function transforms the relationship between the predictors and the target so that it can be modeled using a linear equation, despite the original relationship being nonlinear
    • there is always a canonical link function (determined by the chosen family)
    • in some cases, one may choose a different link to vary how the model is interpreted or to obtain a better model fit

  • The Gaussian, Bernoulli and Poisson distributions are all members of a wider class of distributions, known as the exponential family

  • Other well known members of this family are the exponential distribution, the Gamma family and the negative binomial distribution

  • In general, we can form a regression by modeling the response Y as coming from a particular member of the exponential family, and then transforming the mean of the response so that the transformed mean is a linear function of the predictors

GLM - more discussions

  • in the domain of machine learning, interpreting and understanding are less important than being able to apply the correct GLM form to practical problems and produce useful predictions

  • generalized linear models use a different technique called maximum likelihood estimation (MLE), which finds the parameter values for the specified distribution that are most likely to have generated the observed data

  • The MLE technique is built into GLM modeling software, and usually applies analytical techniques to identify the optimal model parameters by iterating repeatedly over the data rather than finding the correct solution directly
